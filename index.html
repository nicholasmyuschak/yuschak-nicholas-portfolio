DEV/DESIGN

Suzanne Collins Site

SMAD 404, Advanced Interactive Design, is one of the two capstone classes at JMU for Interactive Design Concentrators. The course is designed to be a culmination of all the skills students have learned as designers and developers.


For this project I was tasked with redesigning and deploying the website for Hunger Games Author: Suzanne Collins within 3 Sprints. Find out more about the project below.

Project Scope


This redesign was a friendly competition between my colleagues and myself to come up with the best design and prototype for suzannecollinsbooks.com. When it was pitched, the main guidelines from the client was that Collins wanted the site to closer match the aesthetic of The Hunger Games Movies, a request for updated navigation, a better way to display her works and a way to purchase those works. The competition was structured using the Agile project management methodology, very common in web development. In Sprint 1 I conducted Diagnostic Usability testing with 5 participants. I wanted my design to be directly inspired by users’ pain points and frustrations. Unfortunately, on this site… there were a few. After gathering data from my participants I was able to consolidate a list of features I’d implement to make the site more user-friendly:

(I first made a moodboard to get an understanding of the current site)

Pain Point 1


Confusing navigation and buried pages. The original design had an “all works” page with all of Collins’ books listed out in random order. Then, each subheading for each book took the user to another individual subpage for that work (which I didn’t even realize until I started conducting my research.)

Pain Point 2


On her current site there was nowhere to buy her works, only a quick links section which brought you to the homepage of Barnes and Noble, not to the page of the book itself. During usability testing, this frustrated participants.

Pain Point 2


Crowded layout with poor information hierarchy and “paralyzing” amount of text. (One of my participants said: “I’m not reading any of this.”) Collins has good information on her site, but there’s too much. From an excessive amount of testimonials to multi-paragraph descriptions and scattered quotes, it was a UX nightmare.

The Redesign

Once I knew what features I’d focus on, I turned my attention to personal research. I had never made an author’s site before, so it was new challenge for me. I needed to see what other successful authors were doing. I paroozed google for about 2 hours, looking at other authors’ sites for inspiration. This was one of the hardest parts of the process for me, but it was also the most useful because it helped me pinpoint what was necessary for authors. I made a few conclusions:


1.) Branding is essential — as an author, you aren’t just selling your works but selling your brand. This is an area where suzannecollinsbooks.com needed desperate help. I made a logo for Collins, picked a color scheme that matched the movies, and made sure there was continuity between these two elements.


2.) Prominent display of works on landing page — this is one of the first things users should see, especially if you have a new work out… you want to prominently display it. I designed a section for her newest work, and put the rest of her works into a multi-item carousel on the landing page.

INTERACTIVE GRAPHICS PROGRAMMING WITH p5.js

ICS Bus Animation

The p5.js library was my first introduction to Javascript, and really helped me fall in love with the language. Like jQUery, p5.js has a library of functions that allow the user to program graphics on the HTML Canvas in a more streamlined manner.


This was one of my first sketches in p5 -- my first attempt at animation. This sketch was a study in objects, global variables and boolean statements. It's also illsutrative of two important functions in p5 that can make for really dynamic experiences: mouseX() (dynamically follows the X position of your mouse) and mouseIsPressed()


In p5, the draw function loops continuously at framerate the user can control. Therefore, it's continually reading where the mouse is and dynamically assigning a value to the variable that determines the bus's position on the x-axis.

Animated Snoopy Portrait

This sketch was a lot more difficult to implement. The most time consuming part was coding each body part of snoopy one by one! I made this before learning about classes and modular programming using functions. If I were to go back and redo it, I'd execute the code a little differently.


Nevertheless, I'm really proud of how it turned out, as I'm a huge fan of snoopy. To accomplish the rain animation in the background I used the p5 random() function to dynamically generate rain drops within constrained coordinates on the canvas.
